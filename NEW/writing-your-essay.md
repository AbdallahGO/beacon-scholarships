# Writing your essay

The essay is the one place in your application where the committee meets *you* — not your grades, not your certificates. You.

There are two ways to use this page. If you're applying on your own, this guide is free — use it and write something true. If you've booked a ticket, this is exactly how our team will build the essay **with** you during your three days.

---

## What committees are actually looking for

Not perfect English. Not a dramatic life story. Committees read hundreds of essays, and the ones they remember do four simple things:

- Show a **genuine reason** for wanting this field — not a generic one.
- Show **evidence**: one or two real moments that prove your interest, not a list of adjectives about yourself.
- Show **fit**: why this program, this country, this scholarship — specifically.
- Show a **direction**: what you intend to do with the opportunity.

## A structure that works

1. **Open with a moment, not a definition.** Skip "Since the dawn of time, engineering has..." Start where your story actually starts — a problem you saw, a question you couldn't drop, a day something clicked.
2. **Tell your story with one or two concrete examples.** A project you built, a difficulty you worked through, a person you helped. Specific beats impressive.
3. **Connect it to this program.** Name what this scholarship offers that fits your path. Show you did your homework.
4. **End with direction.** What will you do with this education — for yourself, and for the people around you?

## Mistakes that quietly kill good essays

- Copying a template — committees have read that exact essay a thousand times.
- Repeating your CV in paragraph form. The essay is for what the CV *can't* say.
- Exaggerating. Experienced readers feel it immediately, and it costs you their trust.
- Writing it the night before the deadline. Good essays are rewritten, not written.
